AIWIZN · Patient Care Onboarding Engine · POC v1

Credentialed. Competent.
Confidently Independent.

An immersive, eight-element onboarding pathway for hospital patient care staff — Certified Nursing Assistants, Patient Care Technicians, and Patient Care Assistants — from pre-employment credentialing through 30/60/90-day mastery. Where an item applies to one role only, it is labeled as such. Every checklist, competency, and decision is tracked by COGNITA and synthesized into a placement-ready READINESS profile.

Why Structured Onboarding Matters
Turnover among patient care staff is highest in the first 90 days. A deliberate onboarding pathway — with structured 30/60/90-day checkpoints — targets that cliff directly: it catches struggling staff early, reinforces competence, and turns onboarding into the first stage of a longer onboarding → upskilling → retention continuum.

Pre-Employment
Element 01 · Pre-Employment Phase · Before Day One
Credentialing — The Gate Before the Floor
No patient care staff member reaches a patient until every credential clears. This element confirms licensure, health, and legal eligibility — the foundation everything else is built on.
Compliance GateLicense VerificationOccupational Health
Why This Element Matters
Credentialing is a regulatory and patient-safety gate, not paperwork. An unverified certification, a missed TB screen, or an unresolved exclusion-list hit can void the entire placement. Mark each item as it is confirmed by HR or Occupational Health.

Applied Decision — Chain of Command in Practice
Decision E2-01 · Escalation & Scope
"The Family Has Questions"
COGNITA: Scope awareness · Escalation routing · Follow-through
Chain of CommandScope
SituationMid-shift, a patient's daughter stops you in the hallway. She is upset that her mother "hasn't been turned in hours" and demands to know why the care plan was changed. The RN is in another room.
⬡
Decision E2-01 · How do you respond?
The daughter is waiting. Some of what she's asking is within your role — and some of it is not. What do you do?
COGNITA: Does the CNA act within scope, escalate cleanly, and follow through?
A
Acknowledge her concern, reposition her mother now and document it, and promptly tell the RN the family has care-plan questions so the nurse can address them.
EXPERT: In-scope action + clean escalation + follow-through. Model chain-of-command behavior.
B
Reassure the daughter and explain the medical reasoning behind the care plan yourself to calm her down.
GAP: Interpreting the care plan is outside CNA scope. Risks misinformation and bypasses the RN.
C
Reposition the patient and tell the daughter the nurse will stop by later when she has a moment.
ADEQUATE: Correct task, but vague escalation — the concern may not reach the RN.
D
Tell the daughter she needs to speak to the nurse, and continue on to your other tasks.
GAP: Dismissive, no escalation, patient left un-repositioned.
Teaching Point — Scope & Escalation
A CNA can always act within scope (repositioning, comfort, documentation) and escalate at the same time. Questions about diagnosis, care-plan changes, or medical reasoning belong to the licensed nurse. "Acknowledge, act in scope, escalate clearly" is the safe pattern.

Applied Decision E3-01 — Found on the Floor
Situation · Fall ResponseYou enter Room 12 and find your 82-year-old patient sitting on the floor beside the bed. She is alert and says, "I just slipped." The bed alarm was switched off.
⬡
Decision E3-01 · What are your first actions?
An unwitnessed fall just happened. What do you do first?
COGNITA: Does the CNA protect the patient, avoid moving her, and escalate before transfer?
A
Stay with her, call for help with the call light, do not move her, and notify the RN immediately so she can be assessed before any transfer.
EXPERT: Protect, do-not-move, escalate first. Correct post-fall protocol.
B
Confirm she's alert, help her back into bed, then tell the nurse what happened.
ADEQUATE: Reported - but moving before assessment risks hidden injury.
C
Help her up quickly, settle her in bed, and finish your rounds — she says she feels fine.
GAP: No assessment, no escalation. Unwitnessed falls require RN evaluation.
D
Document the fall and switch the bed alarm back on, then move on to your assignment.
GAP: Charting before notifying the RN delays assessment.
Applied Decision E3-02 — Contact Precautions
Situation · Infection PreventionRoom 8 has a contact-precautions sign for C. difficile. You need to take vital signs and assist the patient with a bedpan.
⬡
Decision E3-02 · How do you proceed?
PPE choice and hand-hygiene method both matter here. What's your approach?
COGNITA: Correct PPE for the precaution type · C. diff hand-hygiene exception
A
Perform hand hygiene, don a gown and gloves before entering, complete care, remove PPE inside the room, and wash with soap and water on exit.
EXPERT: Full PPE + soap-and-water for C. diff. Correct precaution practice.
B
Put on a gown and gloves, complete the tasks, and use the alcohol hand sanitizer on your way out.
ADEQUATE: Correct PPE - but alcohol gel doesn't kill C. diff spores.
C
Wear gloves for the bedpan, take vitals quickly without a gown since you won't be long, and sanitize after.
GAP: Incomplete PPE - contact precautions require a gown.
D
Ask another aide to take it since you already wore PPE in that hallway earlier in the shift.
GAP: Avoidance - each encounter needs fresh, correct PPE.

Applied Decision E4-01 — Dignity & Refusal
Situation · ADLsYour patient, recovering from hip surgery, refuses her morning bath: "Leave me alone, I'm tired."
⬡
Decision E4-01 · What is your best approach?
She has the right to refuse — but skin integrity after hip surgery matters. What do you do?
COGNITA: Respects autonomy · Offers choice · Reports the refusal
A
Respect the refusal for now, offer choices — a partial wash later, or after she rests — explain why hygiene protects her skin, and report the refusal to the RN.
EXPERT: Autonomy + choice + education + escalation. Person-centered care.
B
Explain that everyone is bathed in the morning and gently go ahead, since hygiene is part of her care.
GAP: Overrides consent - the patient has the right to refuse.
C
Tell her you'll return in an hour to try again, and move on to your next patient.
ADEQUATE: Reasonable timing - but no choice offered and refusal not reported.
D
Skip the bath for the day and don't mention it, since she has the right to refuse.
GAP: Refusal not reported - hidden skin-integrity risk.
Applied Decision E4-02 — Safe Patient Handling
Situation · Transfers & MobilityA 250-lb patient on bed rest for three days needs to move to the chair. He is weak and unsteady. The unit has a sit-to-stand lift available.
⬡
Decision E4-02 · How do you transfer him?
Safe handling protects both the patient and you. What's your method?
COGNITA: Equipment selection · Confirms mobility status · Two-person practice
A
Use the mechanical sit-to-stand lift with a second staff member, after confirming his transfer order and mobility status with the RN.
EXPERT: Lift + two-person + confirmed order. Safe-handling standard met.
B
Use a gait belt and have a coworker stand by while you transfer him manually.
ADEQUATE: Stand-by helps - but a lift is the safer choice for this patient.
C
Transfer him yourself with a gait belt — you've handled heavier, and the lift takes too long.
GAP: Solo manual transfer - high injury risk for patient and CNA.
D
Have him hold onto your neck and shoulders and pivot him across to the chair.
GAP: Unsafe - patients must never pull on your neck during a transfer.
Applied Decision E4-03 — An Abnormal Vital Sign
Situation · Vital SignsRoutine vitals on your post-op patient: BP 88/54, HR 116, the rest within normal limits. He looks pale and says he feels "lightheaded."
⬡
Decision E4-03 · What do you do?
The numbers are abnormal and the patient has symptoms. What's your next action?
COGNITA: Confirms the value · Keeps patient safe · Reports without interpreting
A
Recheck the BP to confirm, keep the patient safe in bed, and report the abnormal vitals and his symptoms to the RN right away.
EXPERT: Confirm, protect, escalate immediately. Correct reporting pattern.
B
Document the vitals and mention them to the nurse when you finish your rounds.
ADEQUATE: Charted - but delayed escalation of an abnormal, symptomatic value.
C
Re-take the BP until you get a reading closer to normal, then chart that value.
GAP: Cherry-picking a normal reading falsifies the record.
D
Tell him to drink water and rest — low blood pressure usually isn't serious.
GAP: Interpreting/treating is outside scope; escalation missed.
Applied Decision E4-04 — A Subtle Change
Situation · Observation & ReportingYour patient was chatty yesterday. Today she is quiet, slow to answer, and didn't finish breakfast. Her vital signs are within normal limits.
⬡
Decision E4-04 · What is your best action?
Vitals are normal — but something has changed. What do you do?
COGNITA: Recognizes a mental-status change · Reports despite normal vitals
A
Report the change in her mental status and intake to the RN promptly and clearly — even though her vitals are normal.
EXPERT: Reports a behavior-baseline change despite normal vitals. CNA as bedside eyes.
B
Note the low intake on the I/O sheet and plan to mention it at the end of shift.
ADEQUATE: Logs intake - but under-reports a same-day mental-status change.
C
Assume she's just tired or having an off day and continue your assignment.
GAP: Dismisses a change in baseline - a potentially significant finding.
D
Ask the family if she's "always like this" and decide from their answer whether to report it.
GAP: Delays reporting; leans on informal information.
Teaching Point — The CNA Reporting Reflex
Across all four decisions the safe pattern repeats: act within scope, confirm what you see, and report changes promptly to the licensed nurse. CNAs spend the most time at the bedside — what they notice and report is often the earliest signal of a deteriorating patient.

Applied Decision E5-01 — The Late Entry
Situation · Flowsheet ChartingIt's near the end of your shift and you realize you took Mr. Lee's 1400 vital signs but never charted them. You clearly remember the values.
⬡
Decision E5-01 · How do you chart them?
The vitals are real and accurate — but the timing matters. What do you do?
COGNITA: Uses the late-entry policy · Records actual vs entry time · No fabrication
A
Chart the vitals now as a late entry, documenting the actual time taken (1400) and the time entered, per the late-entry policy.
EXPERT: Proper late entry - actual time + entry time. Honest and policy-compliant.
B
Chart them under the current time so the flowsheet looks complete.
ADEQUATE: Values recorded - but inaccurate timing; not a true late entry.
C
Leave them blank and just tell the next shift the values verbally.
GAP: Incomplete legal record - verbal report isn't documentation.
D
Estimate slightly different values since you're not 100% sure, just to be safe.
GAP: Estimating values is fabrication - never chart a guess.
Applied Decision E5-02 — Family-Reported Intake
Situation · Intake / OutputA patient's son tells you, "She drank two full cups of juice while you were on break." You did not witness it.
⬡
Decision E5-02 · How do you document the intake?
The intake is relevant, but you didn't measure it. What do you chart?
COGNITA: Documents source honestly · Doesn't omit or misattribute data
A
Document the intake as reported by the family, clearly noting it was family-reported, and let the RN know.
EXPERT: Charted with source noted + RN informed. Honest and complete.
B
Add roughly 480 mL to the I/O sheet as though you measured it yourself.
ADEQUATE: Intake captured - but recorded as measured when it wasn't.
C
Don't chart it at all, since you didn't see it and can't be certain.
GAP: Omits relevant intake data - chart it with the source instead.
D
Ask the son for a number and chart whatever he says, without noting the source.
GAP: Unsourced data charted as fact.
Teaching Point — Documentation Integrity
Two rules cover almost every CNA charting decision: chart accurately and chart honestly. Record what was actually done, at the actual time, and note the source of any information you did not observe yourself. Never guess, never backfill, never misattribute.

Element 06 · Unit Orientation · Assigned Floor
Unit-Specific — Every Floor Is Its Own World
Workflow, patient population, routines, and communication rhythms differ on every unit — and an aide's day-to-day responsibilities on Med-Surg differ from those of a colleague in the ED or ICU. A nursing assistant who is excellent on one unit still needs deliberate orientation to the next.

High Acuity

Specialty

Older adults, often frail; some patients have cognitive impairment, and fall risk is elevated. Patience, validation, redirection, and dignity-centered care.
Applied Decision E6-01 — A Geriatrics Unit Moment
Situation · Geriatrics UnitA resident with dementia is anxiously trying to leave the unit, saying she "has to pick up her children from school."
⬡
Decision E6-01 · What is your best response?
This unit calls for a different communication style than Med-Surg. How do you respond?
COGNITA: Validation vs reality orientation · De-escalation · Exit-seeking escalation
A
Approach calmly, validate her feeling, gently redirect with a reassuring conversation or activity, and let the RN know she is seeking to exit.
EXPERT: Validate + redirect + escalate exit-seeking. Dementia-care standard.
B
Gently correct her — explain her children are grown and she lives here now — then walk her back.
ADEQUATE: Well-meant - but reality orientation often increases distress in dementia.
C
Tell her firmly that she cannot leave and guide her back to her room.
GAP: Escalates agitation; can feel restraining.
D
Let her walk with you and ignore the topic until she forgets about it.
GAP: Leaves her anxious; exit-seeking not escalated to the RN.
Teaching Point — Unit Fit Is a Skill
The same patient situation calls for different responses on different units. Unit-specific orientation is not a formality — it teaches the population's safety risks, communication style, and routines so the the staff member's general skills land correctly on that specific floor.

Applied Decision E7-01 — Speaking Up Near the Finish Line
Situation · End of Buddy PeriodYour preceptor is busy and asks you to transfer a patient using the mechanical (Hoyer) lift — a piece of equipment you have not yet been checked off on.
⬡
Decision E7-01 · What do you do?
You're almost independent — but this skill isn't validated yet. What's your move?
COGNITA: Scope honesty · Psychological safety to speak up · Patient-safety priority
A
Politely tell your preceptor you haven't completed the mechanical-lift check-off, and ask her to supervise you or assign it to a checked-off staff member.
EXPERT: Honest about the gap; asks for supervision. Safe speak-up behavior.
B
Do it yourself — you've watched it several times and don't want to seem unprepared.
ADEQUATE: Watched it is not validated. Performing an unchecked skill is a risk.
C
Operate the lift yourself and chart the transfer without mentioning you weren't checked off.
GAP: Un-validated equipment used; the gap concealed.
D
Quietly skip the transfer and hope the next shift catches it.
GAP: Assigned care omitted with no communication.
Teaching Point — Progress at the Pace of Competence
The buddy period exists so a CNA never has to choose between looking ready and being ready. Saying "I haven't been checked off on that yet" is a sign of a safe new CNA — and a well-run preceptorship treats it as exactly that.

Applied Decision E8-01 — The 60-Day Check-In
Situation · 60-Day ReviewAt her 60-day review, a CNA admits she still feels uncomfortable using the mechanical lift and has been quietly avoiding patients who need it.
⬡
Decision E8-01 · What is the best response from the program?
A checkpoint just surfaced a real gap. How should it be handled?
COGNITA: Treats the checkpoint as a safety-net · Targeted re-education · No punitive framing
A
Thank her for the honesty, schedule a targeted lift re-validation with a preceptor, and follow up at 90 days — with no punitive framing.
EXPERT: Honest disclosure met with targeted re-education. The checkpoint working as designed.
B
Reassure her she'll get more comfortable with time and encourage her to keep practicing on her own.
ADEQUATE: Supportive - but the skill gap and avoidance go unaddressed.
C
Issue a written warning for avoiding assigned patients and note it in her file.
GAP: Punishes honest disclosure - teaches CNAs to hide gaps.
D
Note the concern, mark the review complete, and move on — she's past her buddy period now.
GAP: Known safety gap left unaddressed - checkpoint wasted.
Teaching Point — Checkpoints Are a Safety Net, Not a Verdict
Structured 30/60/90-day reviews exist to catch struggles while they are still small and fixable. When a checkpoint surfaces a gap, the right response is targeted re-education — not punishment. Programs that respond well retain more staff and prevent more harm.
